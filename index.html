1. Introduction to OT (Q1-10)

This section covers the foundational concepts of Operational Technology and its critical distinction from Information Technology.

1. What is Operational Technology (OT)?

Operational Technology (OT) is the hardware and software used to monitor and control physical processes and devices. It directly manages physical systems in industrial environments.

Examples: Controlling a factory's assembly line, managing a power grid, or operating a water treatment plant.

2. How is OT different from Information Technology (IT)?

The main difference is their focus and goals:

  • IT: Focuses on managing **data**. The main goal is **Confidentiality**, then Integrity and Availability.
  • OT: Focuses on managing **physical processes**. The main goal is **Availability** (to keep things running), then Integrity and Confidentiality.
3. Why is OT becoming a new target for cyberattacks?

OT systems, which were once isolated, are now connected to IT networks and the internet. This connection makes them vulnerable to attacks from the outside, turning them into a high-value target for hackers who want to disrupt critical services.

4. What is a "safety instrumented system" (SIS) in an OT environment?

An SIS is a special, automated system designed to bring an industrial process to a safe state during an emergency. It acts as an emergency shutdown system to prevent accidents, so its security is extremely important for human safety.

5. Why are legacy OT systems a major security risk?

Many OT systems are old and were built before cyber threats were a concern. They lack modern security features like encryption, strong passwords, and easy updates. This makes them very vulnerable to new attacks.

6. How are OT communication protocols different from IT protocols?

OT protocols (like Modbus and DNP3) are designed for simple, fast communication over industrial networks. They often lack built-in security features, unlike modern IT protocols (like TCP/IP) which are designed for the internet and include security.

7. Why is "physical security" so important in OT?

Physical security is key because many OT attacks involve a hacker getting physical access to a device. By controlling who can physically enter a factory floor or a control room, you can prevent many types of attacks, like planting malware via a USB drive.

8. What is the main purpose of "asset inventory" in OT security?

An asset inventory is a complete list of all the hardware and software in an OT environment. It is a fundamental security step because you cannot protect what you don't know you have. It helps you find old, unpatched systems and unauthorized devices.

9. What is a "DMZ" and how is it used in OT security?

A **Demilitarized Zone (DMZ)** is a small, separate network that acts as a secure buffer between the IT network and the OT network. It prevents direct contact between the two, forcing all communication to go through a monitored and controlled security zone.

10. What is a "human machine interface" (HMI)?

An HMI is a user interface that allows an operator to interact with and control an industrial process. It's the screen that shows the status of a machine and lets the operator send commands, like starting or stopping a pump.

2. OT Security Architecture (Q11-20)

This section focuses on the frameworks and models used to design and secure OT networks, including risk assessment and compliance.

11. What is the Purdue Enterprise Reference Model and why is it important?

The Purdue Model is a framework that divides an industrial control system network into logical layers, from the factory floor (Level 0) to the enterprise IT network (Level 5). It's important because it provides a blueprint for segmenting a network and placing security controls at each level to prevent attacks from spreading.

12. Explain the concept of "defense in depth" for OT security.

Defense in depth is a security strategy that uses multiple layers of security controls. If one layer fails, another layer is there to provide protection. In OT, this includes using firewalls, network segmentation, strong access controls, and physical security.

13. What is the difference between a vulnerability and a threat in OT security?

A **vulnerability** is a weakness in a system that can be exploited (e.g., an unpatched software bug). A **threat** is a potential cause of harm, like a hacker or a piece of malware.

14. What are the key steps in an OT security risk assessment?

The key steps include:

  • **Identifying assets:** Knowing what you have to protect.
  • **Identifying threats & vulnerabilities:** Finding out who might attack you and what your weaknesses are.
  • **Analyzing risk:** Evaluating the chance of an attack and its potential impact.
  • **Implementing controls:** Putting security measures in place to reduce the risk.
15. How does network segmentation improve OT security?

Network segmentation divides the OT network into smaller, isolated zones. This stops an attacker from moving freely across the entire network. If one segment is compromised, the damage is contained and cannot easily spread to more critical areas.

16. Why is it hard to apply traditional IT security patches to OT systems?

Patching an OT system can cause a process to stop, which leads to major financial losses and safety risks. Many OT systems are also old and may not be compatible with modern patches, and applying them could void the warranty from the vendor.

17. Explain the concept of "air-gapping" in OT security.

Air-gapping means physically isolating a network from all other networks, including the internet. This provides the highest level of security against remote cyberattacks, as the only way to breach the network is with physical access.

18. What is the role of the ISA/IEC 62443 standard?

The ISA/IEC 62443 standard is a set of rules and guidelines for securing industrial automation and control systems. It provides a full framework for managing OT security risks and is a key benchmark for companies worldwide.

19. Why is a risk assessment crucial for OT environments?

A risk assessment helps an organization understand its most critical assets, the threats they face, and how likely an attack is. It helps prioritize security investments to protect what matters most, rather than spending money on less critical areas.

20. How does a firewall's role in an OT network differ from an IT network?

In IT, a firewall often blocks malicious traffic based on IP addresses and ports. In OT, a firewall must also understand industrial protocols to allow only specific, approved commands to pass between zones, ensuring that operations are not disrupted.

3. Vulnerability Analysis & Threats (Q21-30)

This section details the common types of attacks that target OT systems, from malware to unauthorized access and supply chain attacks.

21. What is the primary goal of an OT cyberattack?

The primary goal is not just to steal data, but to **disrupt, manipulate, or destroy a physical process**. This can lead to damaged equipment, stopped production, or even risks to human life.

22. Explain what a "supply chain" attack is in an OT context.

A supply chain attack involves a hacker injecting malicious code into hardware or software during its manufacturing or distribution. An attacker could compromise a vendor's software update, which is then unknowingly installed on many OT systems, giving the attacker a backdoor into the network.

23. How does ransomware affect OT systems differently than IT?

While ransomware in IT encrypts data, in OT it often targets operational processes. A hacker can use ransomware to shut down a manufacturing line or a power plant and demand a ransom to restore operations, causing physical disruption rather than just data loss.

24. What is a "zero-day" vulnerability?

A zero-day vulnerability is a software flaw that is unknown to the vendor and has no available patch. Hackers can exploit this weakness before anyone knows it exists, making it very dangerous.

25. What is the role of social engineering in OT cyberattacks?

Social engineering is the art of tricking people to gain access to a system. An attacker might send a phishing email to an OT engineer, who then clicks a malicious link, bypassing the network's technical defenses and giving the attacker a way into the system.

26. Briefly explain the Stuxnet attack and its significance.

Stuxnet was a malware that targeted Iran's nuclear program. It manipulated industrial systems to cause physical damage to centrifuges while showing operators false data. It was the first attack to prove that malware could cause real-world, physical destruction of OT equipment.

27. What happened in the Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack?

Hackers used ransomware to attack the IT systems of the Colonial Pipeline. Even though the OT systems were not directly hit, the company had to shut down the pipeline to contain the attack, which caused major fuel shortages. This showed how connected IT and OT systems are.

28. What were the key lessons from the Triton/Trisis malware attack?

Triton was malware that targeted **Safety Instrumented Systems (SIS)**. The attack showed that hackers were no longer just trying to disrupt processes, but were actively trying to disable safety systems. This highlighted that securing SIS is crucial to prevent disasters and protect human lives.

29. How did the Ukrainian power grid attacks highlight OT vulnerabilities?

In 2015, hackers used malware to get remote access to the Ukrainian power grid's OT systems and manually shut down substations, causing a large power outage. This was a clear example that OT systems are now a target for cyberattacks that can cause real-world disruption.

30. What is the main takeaway from recent OT attacks?

The main takeaway is that OT security is now a matter of physical and national security. Hackers have the ability to cause physical damage, and OT systems must be secured with the highest level of care to protect critical infrastructure.
